# Business Sourcing Requirements for AI Automation Services

## Purpose

This document defines the requirements a business should meet before being considered a qualified prospect for AI automation, AI agents, workflow automation, or operational efficiency services.

The goal is not to prove from the outside that a company needs automation. The goal is to identify strong external signals that suggest the company likely has repetitive, manual, revenue-impacting workflows worth discussing.

## Ideal Business Profile

A strong target business is one that:

- Handles a meaningful number of customer inquiries, leads, requests, applications, appointments, documents, or support cases.
- Relies on repeatable internal processes that happen daily or weekly.
- Uses email, forms, spreadsheets, CRMs, booking tools, portals, or other digital systems.
- Loses time, money, or opportunities when follow-up, scheduling, quoting, reporting, or data entry is slow.
- Has enough budget to pay for a service that saves time, improves response speed, or increases revenue.
- Has a reachable owner, founder, operator, sales leader, or operations decision maker.

The best targets are usually not the flashiest companies. They are businesses with boring, expensive, repetitive work.

## Core Requirements to Look For

### 1. Clear Operational Pain

The business should show signs of friction, inefficiency, overload, or missed opportunities.

Look for:

- Reviews mentioning poor communication, slow response, missed calls, scheduling confusion, or lack of follow-up.
- Website language around complex processes such as intake, estimates, claims, applications, onboarding, dispatch, or support.
- Multiple customer touchpoints before a sale or service is completed.
- A business model where delays directly affect revenue.

Examples of qualifying pain:

- Leads are not followed up quickly.
- Staff spend too much time entering data manually.
- Customers wait too long for quotes or appointments.
- Documents are collected, reviewed, or processed manually.
- Reports are built by hand from multiple systems.

### 2. Repeatable Workflow

The company should have workflows that happen often enough to justify automation.

Look for:

- Contact forms, quote forms, intake forms, booking pages, or application portals.
- High number of services, products, properties, cases, jobs, or customers.
- Multiple locations or teams.
- Job postings for coordinators, assistants, schedulers, dispatchers, support reps, or operations staff.

Minimum requirement:

- The workflow should happen at least weekly.
- Stronger prospects have workflows that happen daily.

Examples:

- A law firm reviewing new client intake forms.
- A home service business responding to quote requests.
- A property manager handling maintenance requests.
- A staffing agency screening candidates.
- A clinic managing appointment follow-ups.

### 3. Digital or Semi-Digital Process

The business should already use digital inputs or systems. Automation is much easier to implement when the workflow already touches software.

Look for:

- Online forms.
- CRM or lead capture tools.
- Calendar booking.
- Chat widgets.
- Customer portals.
- Email-based workflows.
- Spreadsheets.
- Helpdesk or ticketing systems.
- PDFs or document uploads.

Good signs:

- The website has embedded forms or booking tools.
- The business uses software names in job posts.
- The company has structured intake, quoting, scheduling, or support processes.

Weak signs:

- No website or outdated web presence.
- Only phone-based contact with no visible systems.
- Very little digital activity.

### 4. Enough Volume

The business must have enough activity for automation to matter.

Look for:

- Many Google reviews.
- Frequent social media posts.
- Paid ads.
- Multiple service areas.
- Multiple locations.
- Active hiring.
- Large team size.
- Many listings, properties, cases, appointments, or clients.

Minimum indicators:

- At least 5-10 hours per month of likely manual work.
- Or enough missed revenue that faster follow-up would matter.

Strong indicators:

- Daily inbound leads.
- Dedicated admin or operations staff.
- Multiple people coordinating the same process.
- Reviews or job posts that suggest workload pressure.

### 5. Budget Capacity

The company should be able to afford implementation and ongoing support.

Look for:

- Established business with real revenue.
- Multiple employees.
- Multiple locations.
- Paid advertising.
- Professional website.
- Hiring activity.
- High-ticket services.
- B2B services.
- Industries where each customer or deal has meaningful value.

Strong budget-fit industries:

- Home services.
- Legal services.
- Medical and dental clinics.
- Insurance agencies.
- Real estate and property management.
- Recruiting and staffing.
- Financial, accounting, and bookkeeping firms.
- Logistics and transportation.
- B2B service companies.

Weak budget-fit signs:

- Solo operator with very low volume.
- Hobby business.
- No clear revenue engine.
- Price-sensitive audience.
- Business appears inactive or outdated.

### 6. Decision Maker Access

You need a realistic path to someone who can approve the project.

Look for:

- Owner, founder, CEO, managing partner, operations manager, sales manager, practice manager, or office manager listed publicly.
- Active LinkedIn profiles.
- Direct email or contact form.
- Small to mid-sized company where decision-making is not buried under layers.

Best targets:

- Owner-led businesses.
- Local or regional companies.
- Companies with 5-100 employees.
- Firms where operations or sales leaders are visible online.

Harder targets:

- Large enterprises with long procurement cycles.
- Companies where no decision maker is identifiable.
- Organizations with heavy compliance needs but unclear budget.

### 7. Measurable Business Outcome

The automation should connect to a clear business result.

Look for workflows tied to:

- More booked calls.
- Faster lead response.
- Fewer missed inquiries.
- Less manual admin time.
- Faster quote creation.
- Better customer communication.
- Reduced support workload.
- Cleaner reporting.
- Faster onboarding.
- Fewer errors.

If the outcome cannot be measured, the offer will be harder to sell.

## Strong Qualification Signals

A company becomes a strong prospect when several of these signals appear together:

- They receive inbound leads through forms, calls, chats, or booking pages.
- Their reviews mention communication delays or follow-up issues.
- They are hiring admin, support, sales, or operations staff.
- Their website shows multiple services, locations, or customer workflows.
- They use digital systems that can connect to automation.
- Their service has high customer value.
- They advertise or actively market online.
- They have visible decision makers.
- Their work involves repetitive intake, scheduling, quoting, reporting, or document handling.

## Disqualification Criteria

Avoid or deprioritize companies that:

- Have no clear repetitive workflow.
- Have very low customer or lead volume.
- Have no visible budget.
- Cannot identify a decision maker.
- Operate almost entirely offline.
- Have no digital systems or usable data.
- Want AI only because it sounds trendy.
- Need complex enterprise compliance before a simple pilot is possible.
- Expect a fully autonomous system for risky decisions with no human review.
- Cannot explain what process they want improved.

### Likely Workflow to Automate

Write one sentence:

This company likely needs help with [workflow] because [external evidence].

Example:

This company likely needs help with lead follow-up because they run paid ads, offer emergency services, have multiple service areas, and several reviews mention slow callbacks.

## Lead Scoring Rubric

Score each category from 1 to 5.

1 = weak signal  
3 = moderate signal  
5 = strong signal

| Category | Score |
| --- | --- |
| Clear operational pain | 1-5 |
| Repeatable workflow | 1-5 |
| Digital or semi-digital process | 1-5 |
| Enough volume | 1-5 |
| Budget capacity | 1-5 |
| Decision maker access | 1-5 |
| Measurable business outcome | 1-5 |

### Score Interpretation

- 30-35: Excellent prospect. Prioritize outreach.
- 24-29: Strong prospect. Worth personalized outreach.
- 18-23: Possible prospect. Contact if list volume is needed.
- Below 18: Low priority. Skip unless there is a specific strategic reason.

## Research Sources to Check

Use these sources when sourcing companies:

- Company website.
- Google Business Profile.
- Google reviews.
- LinkedIn company page.
- LinkedIn employee profiles.
- Indeed, ZipRecruiter, or company career pages.
- Facebook, Instagram, or other active social channels.
- BuiltWith or similar tools for technology clues.
- Local business directories.
- Industry association directories.
- Paid ad presence when visible.

## Outreach Angle Requirements

Do not reach out with a generic AI pitch. Every sourced business should have a specific reason for contact.

A strong outreach angle includes:

- The workflow you believe may be costing them time or money.
- The external evidence that led you to that hypothesis.
- The business outcome you can improve.
- A simple offer to explore whether it is actually a problem.

Example angle:

I noticed your team handles online quote requests across several service areas. Companies with that setup often lose revenue when follow-up is delayed or inconsistent. I help businesses automate lead follow-up and appointment coordination so new inquiries get handled faster without adding more admin work.

## Final Qualification Rule

From the outside, qualify for probability.

On the discovery call, qualify for proof.

A business is worth sourcing when the public evidence suggests:

- Pain is likely.
- The workflow repeats.
- The process is digital enough.
- The company has enough volume.
- The company can pay.
- A decision maker can be reached.
- The outcome can be measured.

If most of those are true, the company belongs on the prospect list.
